A Reflection on McLuhan, M. ‘The Medium is the Message'

Today I read a paper called The Medium is the Message by Marshall McLuhan. Though the piece is representative of its time in a few ways, such as treatment of Africans as universally illiterate, it is also foresightful in its conception of “electric media” as a force of significant societal change, and thoughtful in considering the connection inherent between the medium of transmission and the message within. After reading, I was aware of a significant prediction in the piece. McLuhan mentions that “each of the media is also a powerful weapon with which to clobber other media and other groups. The result is that the present age has been one of multiple civil wars that are not limited to the world of art and entertainment” (McLuhan, 1964).

This has proven true in every new form of media we have created since McLuhan wrote this paper, perhaps even progressively so, with the newest forms of media, social media, featuring extraordinary amounts of direct conflict (Anderson, 2018) (Leighton, 2019). It is also the case that this trend of increasing conflict follows the trend of increasing interactability of new media. If this understanding is correct, then over time our conflicts in the real world may continue to be superseded by conflicts in the progressively interactable realm of digital media. It may be the case then, that as these new media become the “technological staples”, the “unique cultural flavor” (McLuhan, 1964) they bring may be one of division and discord.

The Memex and the Message

Vannevar Bush’s paper As We May Think was written half a century before his ideas manifested in the World Wide Web. On its 65th anniversary, the paper was reviewed by Simon Harper in ‘As We May Think’ At 65, discussing the value and predictive power it holds even today. Noted in Harper’s paper, is Bush’s intention for the Memex to be a mechanical device, elucidating the fact that all technologies Bush discussed take a significantly different physical form today than he predicted. Nonetheless, ideas and insights from his paper remain deeply relevant, such as the idea that the Memex would be a “device for individual use”, adapted to its user and their specific needs. Harper makes this idea explicit, saying “The key point here is the idea that human understanding and cognition can be enhanced by tailoring information, and the associations between this information, to the way that the human brain is organised”.

Online services such as Spotify, Netflix, and Facebook have taken this concept and applied it to human experience, enhancing each user’s time on their platform by providing content tailored to the individual. In the case of Netflix, different users are shown different cover art for the same shows and movies depending on a digital psychological profile created by algorithmic processing of their viewing habits, with the purpose of providing quality information about each show or movie to users who process information uniquely. The contrast of Bush’s spot on “vision of how information could be manageable even on a large scale by association based on the experience of the individual” and his far less accurate idea of the medium through which that information would pass calls into question the strength of the idea that the medium is the message.

I have discussed Marshall McLuhan’s idea of the message being inseparable from the message on this blog before. McLuhan provides historical evidence to back up his theory, such as the effect of print media on the French Revolution. Yet, looking back from McLuhan’s perspective, and forward from Bush’s, it seems plausible that the medium does not so much affect the message as it enables new kinds of messages to be popularized. Bush predicted the individualisation of the message not because his preferred medium of “rods, gears, pulleys and rotors” imbues information with an essence of the individual, but because computation allows fundamentally new kinds of messages to be transmitted. Perhaps print only provided the ideas of liberté, égalité, and fraternité a mode of transmission, and had radio instead of print emerged as the technology of transmission at the time, a very similar French Revolution would have occurred, with the same message of liberté, égalité, and fraternité as its engine.
